DEFINITION OF READY (DoR) - INDUSTRY BEST PRACTICES STANDARD
=============================================================

Version: 1.0
Date: 2026-06-15
Sources: Scrum Guide 2020, INVEST Criteria (Bill Wake, 2003), SAFe 6.0,
         Professional Scrum Developer Guidelines, Agile Alliance


1. PURPOSE OF DEFINITION OF READY
----------------------------------

The Definition of Ready (DoR) is a team-level agreement that specifies
the criteria a Product Backlog Item (PBI) must satisfy before the team
accepts it into a Sprint. It ensures:

- Shared understanding between Product Owner and Development Team
- Reduced waste from mid-sprint clarification and rework
- Predictable sprint delivery and sustainable pace
- Clear accountability for preparation vs. execution

The DoR answers: "Can we start this work with confidence that we have
everything needed to complete it within the sprint?"


2. ESSENTIAL DoR AREAS (10 CRITERIA)
-------------------------------------

A comprehensive Definition of Ready should address ALL of the following
areas. Each area represents a dimension that, when missing, creates risk
of mid-sprint blockers, scope creep, or delivery failure.

2.1 USER STORY / REQUIREMENT CLARITY
   - The requirement is clearly articulated and understandable by any
     team member without additional explanation
   - Written in user story format ("As a [role], I want [goal], so that
     [benefit]") OR as a clear technical requirement statement
   - Context and business value are explicit
   - The "why" behind the work is documented

2.2 ACCEPTANCE CRITERIA (AC)
   - Specific, testable, and measurable acceptance criteria are defined
   - AC follows a structured format (Given/When/Then, numbered list,
     or verifiable checklist)
   - Each criterion is independently verifiable (pass/fail)
   - Edge cases and boundary conditions are addressed
   - AC is confirmed/agreed with the team

2.3 ESTIMATION / SIZING
   - The item is small enough to be completed within a single sprint
   - If too large, it has been decomposed into smaller items
   - Complexity, uncertainty, and effort are factored into estimates

2.4 DEPENDENCIES IDENTIFIED AND RESOLVED
   - All internal dependencies (other teams, other backlog items) are
     identified and documented
   - External dependencies (third-party APIs, vendor deliverables,
     infrastructure) are identified
   - Blocking dependencies are resolved BEFORE sprint start, or a
     clear resolution plan with timeline exists
   - Points of contact for dependencies are identified

2.5 DESIGN / UX / TECHNICAL SPECIFICATION
   - UI/UX mockups or wireframes are finalized and available (if the
     item involves user-facing changes)
   - Technical approach is discussed and agreed (architecture decisions
     documented if non-trivial)
   - TechSpec reviewed and approved by architect (if required by team)
   - API contracts or interface definitions are available (if applicable)
   - For backend/infrastructure work: system design is clear

2.6 SCOPE / SPRINT FIT
   - The item's scope is clearly bounded (what is IN and what is OUT)
   - The item can realistically be completed within a single sprint
   - No open questions that could expand scope remain unresolved
   - "Done" is clearly understood for this item
   - If scope changes are needed, a new item is created

2.7 RISKS AND BLOCKERS IDENTIFIED
   - Known risks are documented with mitigation strategies
   - Potential blockers are identified and either resolved or have
     contingency plans
   - Technical uncertainty is acknowledged and bounded (spike may be
     needed for high-uncertainty items)
   - Compliance, security, or regulatory risks are flagged

2.8 STAKEHOLDER ALIGNMENT / PO APPROVAL
   - Product Owner has reviewed and prioritized the item
   - Business stakeholders have validated the requirements
   - There is no ambiguity about what "success" looks like
   - The item is aligned with current sprint/release goals
   - Any required approvals (legal, compliance) are obtained

2.9 TECHNICAL FEASIBILITY CONFIRMED
   - The team has confirmed the work is technically feasible with
     current tools, infrastructure, and skills
   - Required access, permissions, and environments are available
   - No infrastructure prerequisites are missing
   - Performance requirements are understood and achievable
   - Engineering investigation or spike completed (if needed)

2.10 TESTING STRATEGY / APPROACH
   - Test approach is identified (unit tests, integration tests,
     manual testing, E2E)
   - Test data and test environments are available or planned
   - Non-functional requirements (performance, security) have
     identified validation methods
   - Regression impact is understood


3. INVEST CRITERIA FOR STORY READINESS
---------------------------------------

Stories should satisfy the INVEST model (Bill Wake, 2003):

- Independent: Can be delivered without tight coupling to other items
- Negotiable: Flexible and open to discussion, not a fixed contract
- Valuable: Delivers clear business or customer value
- Estimable: Can be sized or forecasted with reasonable confidence
- Small: Fits within a single sprint
- Testable: Can be validated through clear acceptance criteria


4. QUALITY CHARACTERISTICS OF A GOOD DoR
------------------------------------------

A high-quality DoR document itself should exhibit these properties:

4.1 SPECIFICITY
   - Criteria are concrete and unambiguous
   - Avoids vague terms like "appropriate", "sufficient", "if needed"
     without defining what triggers the condition
   - Each criterion has a clear verification method

4.2 MEASURABILITY
   - Each criterion can be evaluated as pass/fail
   - Team members would independently agree on whether a criterion is met
   - No subjective interpretation required

4.3 ACTIONABILITY
   - The DoR guides behavior (refinement activities, preparation steps)
   - Criteria are achievable within normal refinement cadence

4.4 COMPLETENESS
   - Addresses different work types (stories, bugs, tasks, spikes)
   - Includes both functional and non-functional considerations

4.5 PROPORTIONALITY
   - Not overly restrictive (doesn't block valuable work)
   - Not too permissive (doesn't allow ambiguous work into sprint)
   - Includes "conditional" criteria (e.g., "if UI changes: mockups needed")
   - Balances thoroughness with agility

4.6 TEAM OWNERSHIP
   - Created and maintained by the team (not imposed externally)
   - Regularly reviewed and updated based on retrospective learnings
   - All team members understand and agree to the DoR


5. COMMON ANTI-PATTERNS
-------------------------

5.1 TOO VAGUE
   - "Requirements are clear" (clear to whom? how verified?)
   - "Acceptance criteria defined" (without specifying quality/format)
   - "Dependencies resolved" (without defining what "resolved" means)

5.2 TOO RIGID
   - Requiring full technical specification for every item regardless
     of complexity
   - Mandating user story format for all work types (ops, spikes)
   - No conditional criteria (everything applies to everything)

5.3 INCOMPLETE
   - Only covers requirements/AC but ignores estimation, dependencies,
     testing
   - No mention of scope boundaries or sprint fit
   - No stakeholder/PO validation step

5.4 NOT ACTIONABLE
   - Lists what should exist but not who provides it
   - No connection to refinement process or workflow
   - No clear "not ready" consequence defined

5.5 COPY-PASTE
   - Generic checklist copied from internet without team customization
   - Does not reflect team's actual domain, tooling, or workflow
   - Never reviewed or updated since creation


6. DoR BY WORK TYPE
--------------------

Different work types may require different readiness criteria:

6.1 USER STORIES / TASKS
   - Full DoR applies (all 10 areas)
   - AC is mandatory and must be specific
   - UX mockups required if user-facing

6.2 BUG 
   - Reproduction steps documented
   - Expected vs. actual behavior described
   - Severity and priority assessed
   - Root cause identified (or investigation bounded)


6.3 SPIKES
   - Time-box defined
   - Questions to answer are explicit
   - Expected output format defined (document, prototype, decision)
   - May be exempt from AC, testing, and estimation criteria


7. ASSESSMENT SCORING GUIDE
-----------------------------

When evaluating a team's DoR quality, assess across these dimensions:

COVERAGE (25% weight):
   Score based on how many of the 10 Essential Areas (Section 2) the
   DoR addresses. 10/10 = 100%, 5/10 = 50%, etc.

CLARITY & SPECIFICITY (20% weight):
   Score based on how specific and unambiguous the criteria are.
   100% = every criterion is concrete and verifiable
   50% = mix of specific and vague criteria
   0% = all criteria are generic/copy-paste

MEASURABILITY (15% weight):
   Score based on whether each criterion has a clear pass/fail check.
   100% = all criteria are objectively verifiable
   50% = some criteria are subjective
   0% = criteria cannot be consistently evaluated

COMPANY STANDARD ALIGNMENT (15% weight):
   Score based on alignment with company-level DoR guidance.
   100% = fully aligned with company DoR principles
   50% = partially aligned
   0% = contradicts or ignores company guidance

INDUSTRY BEST PRACTICES ALIGNMENT (10% weight):
   Score based on adherence to INVEST, Scrum Guide, and SAFe guidance.
   100% = exemplifies industry best practices
   50% = follows some practices
   0% = contradicts established practices

ACTIONABILITY (10% weight):
   Score based on whether DoR drives specific behaviors.
   100% = clear ownership, workflow integration, consequences
   50% = some actionable guidance
   0% = passive checklist with no behavioral guidance

AC GUIDANCE (5% weight):
   Score based on how well DoR addresses acceptance criteria quality.
   100% = specific format requirements, quality standards for AC
   50% = mentions AC must exist
   0% = no mention of AC quality or format
